## Description

Developers evaluating a new tool are highly sensitive to upfront friction. Tools that require an account, a cloud service, a billing decision, or a team setup before they produce any value rarely survive the first five minutes of evaluation — especially when the user is a solo developer exploring an early-stage project.

Etak's target user often starts alone. They're a founder scoping a new product, a staff engineer proving out a direction, a consultant bootstrapping a client engagement. They don't yet have a team. They don't want a SaaS account. They want to clone a repo, run a command, and start capturing ideas and opportunities while they think. Only later — as the project gains stakeholders, the knowledge base grows, or the team expands — do they need the things cloud and collaboration tiers provide.

The gap today is that most product discovery tooling assumes the team-tier user from day one. Local-first tools exist (Obsidian, plain markdown, spreadsheets), but they have no structured model, no AI integration, and no upgrade path to a collaborative system. Cloud tools exist (Productboard, Vistaly, Linear), but they require the team-tier commitment before they deliver any value to a solo user.

Etak has a specific opportunity here because the underlying artifact model is the same at every stage. The only things that change are where the artifacts live and who can see them. If the CLI abstracts the backend, a solo user can adopt Etak at the lowest-friction tier and grow into higher tiers without ever re-learning the tool or losing their work.

This opportunity is the parent frame for a set of more specific sub-opportunities: the migration path between tiers, the signal for when to upgrade, and the onboarding experience of a first-time user. Those are captured as sibling opportunities under the same objective.

## Evidence

- Developer tool adoption patterns consistently show that local-first, zero-account tools (VS Code, git, ripgrep, Obsidian) have deeper and broader adoption than comparable cloud-first alternatives in the same space.
- The existing Forge skills are already filesystem-oriented — they read and write markdown in the working project. This is an accidental but useful validation that the local tier is a real and usable surface.
- Product-led growth case studies (Figma, Notion, Linear, Vercel) show that the wedge into team adoption is almost always a solo user who becomes an internal advocate. The solo experience is not optional — it's the sales channel.
- Teresa Torres' CDH practice is often introduced by a single practitioner inside a team before it spreads. Etak needs to meet that practitioner where they are, not where their team will be in six months.

## Who Experiences This

- Solo founders in the idea-validation stage, working alone before hiring.
- Staff engineers and tech leads doing discovery work inside larger orgs before involving product managers.
- Consultants and contractors bootstrapping client engagements with no shared infrastructure yet.
- Students and self-taught builders learning the discovery practice without an organizational context.
