## Description

Given a discovery graph with many opportunities, ideas, and assumptions, where should the team spend their next unit of attention? This decision recurs constantly — every sprint, every planning session, every time a new opportunity surfaces. Today it is:

- **Implicit.** The PM picks from gut, or scans a spreadsheet no one else maintains.
- **Shallow.** Teams use impact/effort whiteboard grids that don't survive the session.
- **Political.** The loudest voice in the room wins; quiet voices with good data lose.
- **Non-traceable.** A decision to focus on Idea A over Idea B leaves no record of why, so the same debate happens again next quarter.

This is a specialization of `no-process-guidance-in-discovery-tools` — that opportunity frames the general gap ("the tool doesn't guide the thinking"), this one picks out a specific recurring thinking activity the tool should make first-class: *choosing where to dig next*.

## Evidence

- Teresa Torres (CDH) treats prioritization as a core, recurring activity, not a one-off — but existing tools don't support it as a structured workflow.
- Popular frameworks (RICE, ICE, impact/effort, MoSCoW) are mechanical scorers; they flatten the political dimension that determines which score actually drives a decision.
- Every PM observed in the wild maintains their prioritization in a side spreadsheet because no discovery tool supports it as a first-class activity.

## Where this sits in the discovery rhythm

Prioritization is one recurring **convergent** activity in the broader divergent/convergent rhythm of discovery — see `support-divergent-convergent-rhythm-of-discovery` for the framing. Convergent activities are the moments a team narrows a candidate set into a commitment. They are only half of the rhythm; the candidates they operate on come from divergent activities (brainstorm, opportunity exploration, stakeholder-driven idea generation). A tool that supports prioritization without supporting the generative side of the pairing creates a different failure mode — ranking the same short list forever.
