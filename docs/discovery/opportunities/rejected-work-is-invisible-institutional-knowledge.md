## Description

The most valuable institutional knowledge isn't what a team decided — it's what they considered and rejected, what they tried and learned didn't work, and why. Invalidated assumptions, failed experiments, and abandoned ideas save future team members from re-treading the same ground and help them understand the constraints that shaped current decisions.

Today, this knowledge is too costly to maintain. Teams don't document rejected alternatives because writing it up is overhead, and even when they do (in ADRs, post-mortems, or Confluence pages), it's rarely consulted because finding and reading it is prohibitive. The result: people approach existing features, wonder why they were built a particular way, and walk away rather than risk changing something they don't understand.

If the discovery graph already captures this history as a natural byproduct of the process (ideas move to "rejected," assumptions get "invalidated," experiments complete with results), the cost of creating the record drops to near zero. The remaining challenge is making it accessible — surfacing relevant rejected alternatives and past learnings in context, when someone is making a related decision.

## Evidence

- ADRs capture "alternatives considered" but only for technical decisions, and only when someone takes the time to write one
- Post-mortems are written after incidents but rarely referenced when making future decisions in the same area
- The discovery graph's status lifecycle already tracks rejections and invalidations — the raw data exists, but no tool surfaces it contextually
