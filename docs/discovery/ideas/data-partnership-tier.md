## Description

The commercial and technical shape of the opt-in program under which customers grant Etak the right to *retain* their content (prompts, responses, generated artifacts, code) for training, eval-set construction, deep diagnostic analysis, and long-term learning — in exchange for a pricing discount, increased product value, and a clear, honest statement of what they're agreeing to and what they get back.

This idea is the product surface that turns the `fair-exchange-for-customer-data` opportunity from a value statement into something a customer can sign up for. It assumes the sharpened data ownership stance: ephemeral content-aware processing (shadow mode, LLM judges, internal candidate selection) is allowed for everyone by default under idea F's rules. The partnership tier is specifically about *retention and training* — the category of content use that sits outside the default and requires explicit, compensated consent.

The partnership is structured around three commitments that Etak makes and three decisions that customers make:

**Etak's commitments:**

1. **A real discount.** Not a token gesture. A discount calibrated to the actual value the data provides to Etak's learning loop, visible as a separate line item on the bill, adjustable as the value becomes measurable, and structurally honest about what it is (a payment, not a rebate).
2. **Real control.** Granular scoping, audit visibility, frictionless withdrawal, and specific commitments about what Etak will *never* do with the shared data — written in plain language, held to precisely.
3. **A fully featured default path.** Customers who don't opt in still get the full product. Opting in unlocks the discount and some partnership-tier benefits, but not access to any core functionality. The default is not degraded to coerce opt-in.

**Customer decisions:**

1. **Scope.** What content to share — whole account, specific projects, specific task types, specific sessions. The customer defines this and can change it at any time.
2. **Retention window.** How long Etak may retain the shared content — default, shorter, or indefinite. Short windows are cheaper for Etak to honor and better for customers worried about exposure; long windows unlock more of the deeper use cases and come with larger discounts.
3. **Permitted uses.** Which categories of Etak's use are permitted — training, eval set construction, diagnostic analysis, publication of aggregate insights. Customers can opt into any subset; Etak does not require all-or-nothing.

The program is a commercial product area, not a feature. It requires its own positioning, pricing, contracts (or click-through terms), onboarding, audit tooling, withdrawal semantics, customer-success touchpoints, and ongoing honesty about how it's working. This is why it sits as its own idea rather than being folded into any of the telemetry or experimentation pieces — the infrastructure it depends on is mostly reused from the rest of the stack, but the *product* is distinct.

## Strategic Rationale

The partnership tier is the path Etak uses to access the data that ephemeral processing can't reach. Without it:

- **Deep model tuning is off the table.** Fine-tuning, RLHF, and eval-set construction all require retained content. Without a consented path, Etak can either use public data (much lower signal about how its actual customers work) or can't do those things at all. Both are worse than having a real partnership program.
- **Hard diagnostic cases can't be investigated properly.** When a quality proxy says "something is wrong with the test-plan agent for customer X" and the team needs to look at what actually happened, the partnership tier is the only way they can do that without violating the default-private commitment. Without the program, those cases are investigated through inference and guesswork.
- **The trust story is incomplete.** Having a strong default-private stance is valuable, but having *only* a default-private stance means Etak has no answer for customers who actively want to contribute. The partnership tier is what makes the trust story a positive-sum arrangement rather than a negative-sum one ("we won't take your data") — it gives customers who want to participate a real, valued way to do so.
- **Revenue model is constrained to usage-only.** The partnership discount is, from one angle, a second revenue lever: customers who won't pay full price may stay at the discounted tier for longer, increasing retention and lifetime value even as per-user revenue drops. Without the program, there's no lever to pull for price-sensitive customers other than reducing price across the board.

There's a longer-term rationale too: the customers who opt into the partnership tier are, by self-selection, the customers most invested in Etak's trajectory. They are design partners, dogfooders, power users, advocates. The partnership tier turns that self-selection into a formal relationship Etak can build on — the tier is a recruitment pipeline for the customers who will most help shape the product.

## How It Could Work

- **Tiered consent as the core UX.** Rather than "share everything or nothing," the partnership tier presents tiered options. Tier 1: share prompts and structural outputs, retain for 90 days, small discount. Tier 2: add responses and artifacts, retain for 1 year, larger discount. Tier 3: full content retention, opt into training use, largest discount. Each tier has a plain-language description of what it covers and what it unlocks. The customer picks a tier, not a checklist.
- **Scope is per-project by default.** The customer chooses which projects participate in the partnership tier. A customer can have some projects in the tier (e.g., internal prototyping work) and others completely out (e.g., client deliverables). This matches how most customers actually think about data sensitivity — some work they don't mind sharing, some they do, and the split is usually along project lines.
- **Session-level overrides are available for sensitive cases.** Even within a project that's in the partnership tier, a customer can flag a specific session as "don't retain this one." This is the escape hatch for the case where a normally-shared project has a one-off sensitive moment. The override is a one-click gesture, not buried in settings.
- **Audit dashboard is first-class.** The customer has a real-time view of every piece of content Etak has retained on their behalf. Grouped by project, by task, by date. Each entry shows what was retained, when, under which tier, and what its expiration date is. One-click deletion is always available for any entry; the deletion is immediate and irreversible.
- **Withdrawal has honest semantics.** Withdrawing from the partnership tier stops future retention and (optionally) deletes previously retained content. What it *cannot* do is reach into models that have already been trained on the retained content — once training has happened, the learning is baked into the model weights and cannot be unwound. The program documents this honestly and gives customers the ability to check whether their data has been used in a training run before withdrawing, so they can make an informed decision.
- **Discount calibration is transparent and evolves.** Etak starts with a good-faith estimate of the data's value and calibrates over time. The discount is reviewed quarterly against the actual value the data has produced (measured against the learning loop's outcomes). If the value is higher than initially estimated, the discount increases. If it's lower, Etak documents why and explains — but doesn't quietly reduce the benefit. The direction of adjustment is one-way, or clearly justified when it isn't.
- **Customers get more than the discount.** Each tier unlocks non-monetary benefits: visibility into what was learned from their contribution (on an aggregated basis that doesn't leak cross-customer specifics), early access to improvements their data informed, an optional "design partner" label that surfaces in their account and marks them as contributors. The deeper the tier, the deeper the relationship.
- **"Never" commitments are specific and public.** Etak publishes a list of things it will never do with partnership-tier data. Candidate commitments: never use a customer's shared data to train a model that customer's direct competitors can access; never publish or share shared content outside Etak; never combine shared data across customers in a way that could identify any customer's work; never sell data to third parties; never retain beyond the stated window. The list is stable — adding to it is fine, removing from it is a trust event that requires customer notification and a cooling-off period.
- **Contracts exist for enterprise, click-through for everyone else.** Self-serve customers accept the terms through click-through with clear language and a walk-through. Enterprise customers negotiate custom terms that can include stricter retention, broader never-commitments, dedicated contacts for compliance questions, or segment-specific provisions. Both paths run on the same underlying technical controls.
- **The partnership tier has its own customer-success surface.** Customers in the tier get a clear contact point — not a help desk, an actual relationship — for questions, concerns, and withdrawal requests. Partnership customers are not lost in the same queue as everybody else. This is where the "partnership" part of the name earns its keep.
- **Program integrity is audited publicly.** Once the program is large enough to matter, Etak commits to periodic third-party audits of the technical controls: is retained content actually deleted when customers withdraw? Is the retention window actually honored? Are never-commitments actually in effect? The audit results are published, or at least made available on request to partnership-tier customers. This is what keeps the program honest against future internal pressure to cut corners.

## Why This Could Work

- The customers who want to participate in a program like this exist, and they are disproportionately the ones who would most help Etak improve. Developer-tool products routinely see 5-15% of their user base actively engaged with feedback, data-sharing, or beta programs when the terms are honest. That's enough volume to produce meaningful signal for training and eval work, without requiring mass participation.
- The commercial model is defensible to customers and to investors. The discount is a real cost that Etak absorbs in exchange for real value that Etak captures. That makes it a business transaction, not a marketing gimmick — and business transactions are structurally more honest than marketing gimmicks.
- The technical controls are mostly infrastructure that's already being built for other reasons: consent state is already needed for the content-sharing toggle on in-flow gap capture (idea H), audit dashboards are already needed for data-ownership compliance, retention policies are already needed for the telemetry schema. The partnership tier binds these together into a coherent product, rather than inventing new infrastructure.
- The alternative — not having a partnership tier — is worse. The data Etak can't access on consented terms is either accessed without consent (which destroys the trust posture) or simply unused (which caps what Etak can learn). Neither is tenable long-term. Building the partnership tier well is the least-bad path.

## Open Questions

- **How is the initial discount calibrated?** Etak has to pick a first number before it has data about how valuable the data is. Too low and nobody opts in; too high and the program loses money. Likely start point: match the discount to the approximate revenue difference between Etak's pricing tiers and a credible free competitor — a price low enough to be a real reason to participate, without tanking margins.
- **How is "value of data" measured over time?** The rational discount depends on how much the data is actually contributing to the learning loop. Measuring that contribution (against a counterfactual where the data wasn't available) is itself an open research problem. The program may have to use a proxy — "revenue attributable to the learning loop improvements this quarter" — and accept that the measurement is rough.
- **What happens to retained data when a customer churns?** A customer who cancels their account has already agreed to retention under the partnership tier. Does that agreement survive churn, or does it automatically trigger a delete? Both are defensible; picking deliberately matters. Probably the customer gets a choice at churn time and the default is "delete."
- **How does the program handle derived data?** If Etak trains a model on partnership data and that model produces embeddings or intermediate representations, are those derived artifacts covered by the partnership terms? This is the kind of question lawyers will ask and Etak should have an answer before lawyers start asking.
- **How are competitors defined in the "never train a model a competitor can access" commitment?** The commitment is strong but the definition of "competitor" is fuzzy. Etak needs either a clear definition (probably based on customer-declared competitors) or a broader commitment that sidesteps the definition problem ("we won't train any model that anyone outside Etak can access on your shared content without separate consent").
- **What does the onboarding flow look like?** The partnership tier has to be discoverable by customers who would opt in, without being pushy for customers who wouldn't. This is a real UX design problem: too quiet and nobody finds it; too loud and it feels like the product is nagging for data. The right shape is probably "available and described in settings, mentioned once during onboarding, surfaced again at billing events" — but this needs to be designed properly, not improvised.
- **How does the partnership tier interact with the flag-level content share from idea H?** A customer not in the partnership tier can still grant content access on a per-flag basis. A customer in the partnership tier has content retention under their tier's terms. Does the per-flag share become redundant for partnership customers? Is there a case for it even then (e.g., flags get retained longer than tier terms for diagnostic purposes)? The interaction needs to be clear so both mechanisms stay coherent.
- **How is the program presented to enterprise buyers?** Enterprise security reviews scrutinize data-handling programs harder than any other review. The partnership tier has to be explicable to a security officer in terms they'll find satisfying: specific technical controls, specific commitments, specific audit mechanisms. A program that's intuitively fair to a developer may still fail a procurement review if the language doesn't land. This is a marketing and legal problem as much as a product one.
