## Description

A first-class system for declaring, running, and concluding experiments that compare different ways of executing an agent task on live customer traffic. The harness takes a structured experiment definition — "compare config A to config B on task T across cohort C with guardrails G for at most D days" — and is responsible for everything from routing the right traffic through the right variant, to enforcing the guardrails, to deciding (with statistical discipline) whether and when the experiment can be concluded.

The harness does not decide *what* to experiment on. Humans decide that. The harness makes running experiments a routine, low-risk, well-instrumented activity rather than a per-experiment improvisation. Its job is to enforce — consistently and by default — the practices that make experimentation safe and conclusive: cohort stability, guardrail-based rollback, statistical power, clear stop conditions, and non-interference between concurrent experiments.

Where the gateway (A) decides which variant to run, the telemetry schema (C) captures what happened, and the quality proxy model (D) scores the outcomes — the harness is the system that *orchestrates* all three around a specific question ("is B better than A?") and produces an answer you can make a decision from.

## Strategic Rationale

The harness is where the objective's three preconditions — execution flexibility, content-free measurement, experimentation as first-class — come together into a loop that actually produces decisions. Without it:

- **Every experiment is bespoke.** Each one is built from scratch — routing rules written by hand, cohort stability improvised, guardrails forgotten, analysis done in a notebook that nobody will ever find again. The overhead is so high that experiments don't happen, and when they do happen, they're done badly enough that the decisions built on them are unreliable.
- **The customer safety bar is aspirational.** "Don't harm individual customers" is a rule that only matters if something is enforcing it. Without a harness, the rule is enforced by whoever remembered to write the rollback logic in this particular experiment — which is to say, it isn't.
- **Statistical discipline loses every fight with pressure.** When someone wants a result, they find it — looking at the first cohort that crossed a threshold, calling it "directionally positive," and shipping. A harness that refuses to declare experiments "won" before they have the power to be conclusive is the only thing that consistently resists this pressure.
- **Experiments interfere with each other silently.** Two experiments running at the same time on overlapping cohorts contaminate each other's results. Without a harness that knows about all active experiments, each one is conducted as if it were the only thing happening — and the decisions coming out of it are built on contaminated data.

There's also a compounding rationale: once the harness exists, the cost of running the next experiment drops to near-zero. Teams that have tight experimentation loops run 10x more experiments than teams that don't, not because they're better scientists but because the friction is different. Etak's defensibility depends on getting into the fast-experimentation regime early and staying there.

## How It Could Work

- **Experiments are declarative.** An experiment is a structured artifact — name, hypothesis, task tag, variants, cohort rule, guardrails, minimum sample size, maximum duration, analysis method — that lives in source control and goes through review before it runs. Not a YAML file nobody reads, but a first-class artifact type with templates, validation, and history.
- **Cohort assignment is deterministic per customer.** A customer assigned to variant B for this experiment stays on variant B for the duration. No flipping mid-session, no flipping across sessions, no flipping when the customer reloads the page. Assignment is derived from a hash of (customer ID, experiment ID) so it's reproducible and can be re-derived at analysis time without a separate lookup table.
- **Holdouts are a first-class concept.** Every experiment defines a holdout cohort that stays on the control variant for the duration, plus a global holdout cohort that never sees *any* experimental variant. The global holdout is how Etak detects whether the accumulation of many small experiments is drifting the product in a direction that's good in pieces but bad in aggregate.
- **Guardrails are enforced in real time.** The harness subscribes to the telemetry stream for its active experiments. When a guardrail proxy (error rate, latency, quality score against the profile, customer reaction rate) crosses its threshold, the experiment rolls back the affected variant automatically — no human in the loop, no "let's wait and see." The harness reports the rollback, explains which guardrail tripped, and preserves the data for postmortem.
- **Statistical discipline is enforced at the analysis layer.** The harness computes power *before* the experiment starts and refuses to launch experiments whose design cannot produce a conclusive result in the declared duration. During the experiment, it refuses to show "interim winners" in any way that would encourage peeking. At the end, it classifies the result as one of a small fixed set of outcomes — *A wins*, *B wins*, *inconclusive*, *rolled back* — not a continuous "how positive was it."
- **Concurrent experiments are managed explicitly.** The harness knows about every active experiment and tracks which cohorts they're assigned against. Overlaps that could contaminate each other are either rejected at launch or stratified (customers in both experiments are assigned consistent combinations of variants so the results can be disentangled). This is the orthogonality design problem from any serious experimentation platform, and the harness has to solve it rather than hoping it doesn't come up.
- **Ramp is structured.** The default is to start small (1% or 5%), hold for a short period to confirm the guardrails are being honored, then ramp to the full experimental share (usually 50%). Instant full-traffic launches are available but require an explicit override and a reason. This is what operationalizes "confidence-build before you expose anyone."
- **Results live in source control.** When an experiment concludes, its result — outcome, metrics, sample size, duration, rollback events if any — is written back to the same declarative artifact and committed. Future experiments can look up what happened. Institutional memory is in the repo, not in Slack.
- **Decisions are separate from experiments.** The harness says "B won" or "inconclusive." It does *not* automatically ship B to 100%. A human (or at a later stage, a reviewed automated process) makes the decision to promote the winning variant. This separation matters because "the experiment said so" is not sufficient to justify a production change — context, secondary effects, and side-by-side sanity checks all belong in the decision, and the harness's job is to produce the evidence, not the verdict.

## Why This Could Work

- The pattern is not novel. Everything in the harness exists in some form in mature experimentation platforms (Google's internal systems, Facebook's Planout, Netflix's XP, Optimizely, Statsig, GrowthBook). Etak is adopting a known pattern, not inventing one. What is new is binding it tightly to agent execution via the gateway and to content-free measurement via the schema and quality model — a combination that is specific to AI products and doesn't have a canonical off-the-shelf answer yet.
- The harness depends only on primitives we're already planning to build. The gateway gives it variant execution. The schema gives it event collection. The quality model gives it scoring. The harness is the glue — the smallest new thing that turns those three primitives into a running loop.
- The friction-lowering effect of a well-built harness is large. Teams with good experimentation platforms run dozens of experiments a month; teams without run a few a quarter. The difference is not talent — it is whether the next experiment is an afternoon or a week. For Etak's learning loop to compound, it has to be an afternoon.

## Open Questions

- **How strict is "no peeking"?** Classical frequentist analysis requires that you define the test upfront and don't look until it's done. Sequential methods (always-valid p-values, Bayesian approaches) allow continuous monitoring without inflating false-positive rates. The harness should pick one and commit — hybrid approaches are where statistical mistakes come from.
- **Who can launch an experiment?** A free-for-all is dangerous (untrained engineers running experiments with badly calibrated guardrails); gating every experiment through a review is slow. Some middle ground — templates for common experiment shapes, review required for anything touching untested configs or high-stakes cohorts — probably works, but the boundary needs to be drawn deliberately.
- **How are customer-facing experiments differentiated from infrastructure experiments?** Comparing two prompts is different from comparing two tool-use loops which is different from comparing two models which is different from comparing two underlying providers. Some of these the customer will notice; some they won't. The harness may need to distinguish between them in how it handles ramp, guardrails, and disclosure.
- **What does the opt-in layer look like?** The experimentation opportunity explicitly mentions customers who want to be part of higher-risk experiments in exchange for early access. The harness has to support per-customer cohort overrides ("this customer is always in the canary cohort") without making them a security hole.
- **How are experiments disclosed to customers?** The opportunity takes a position that quietly experimenting on production traffic without disclosure is a procurement blocker. The harness should make disclosure part of the default path — not because every customer wants the details, but because the disclosure mechanism has to exist before the first customer asks.
- **What happens when an experiment's question is ambiguous in retrospect?** Sometimes an experiment that was launched to answer question X turns out to be the most relevant data for answering unrelated question Y. Is that data reusable, or is the harness strict about "this experiment was designed for this question and its data shouldn't be repurposed"? Both answers have costs; picking deliberately matters.
- **How does the harness interact with design partners and dogfood users?** Neal (and whoever else eventually dogfoods Etak) is a customer-but-not-really. Is dogfood traffic part of experiments, exempt from them, or in a special cohort that sees every variant for confidence-building? The answer affects how much signal the early dogfood period can contribute.
- **When does automated promotion become acceptable?** Today the harness proposes and humans dispose. At some point — when the harness has enough history of being right, when guardrails are proven, when the cost of manual review exceeds its value — it may make sense to let the harness auto-promote clear winners on some defined class of experiments. What does that threshold look like? Not urgent, but worth framing now so "we'll figure it out later" doesn't drift into "we never did."
