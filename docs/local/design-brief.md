# Etak — Logo & Brand Mark Design Brief
**Open Brief — Version 2.0 — April 2026**

---

## Overview

This is an open brief. We have done preliminary concept exploration and have strong opinions about direction, but we are explicitly not prescribing a solution. We want a designer to bring genuine craft and judgment to this.

The brief describes the product, the naming concept, the brand character, and two mark directions worth exploring. A suggested color palette is included as a starting point — treat it as a strong hypothesis, not a constraint. A set of AI-generated reference images is appended at the end of this document; they are included only to show where the exploration landed, not to define what the final mark should be. You are welcome to ignore them entirely.

**What we need:** A logo mark and wordmark that can carry a serious, distinctive software brand. We are at the very beginning — this is the founding mark, the thing that will appear in the product nav bar, on the website, in pitch decks, and in every investor conversation we have.

## About Etak

### The product

Etak is a product development platform for software teams. It is the system of record for how product decisions get made — the place where a vague customer observation becomes a structured hypothesis, becomes a validated assumption, becomes shipped code, becomes a measured outcome, becomes institutional memory.

Most product development tools are built around a roadmap: a linear path from backlog to shipped. Etak is built around a different premise. Product development is not a road. It is navigation through a complex, non-linear space. The right tool doesn't flatten that complexity — it makes it legible and navigable.

### The name

Etak is a Polynesian and Micronesian navigational technique practiced most completely in the Caroline Islands. It is the cognitive method that allowed human beings to navigate the Pacific Ocean — the largest, most featureless space on earth — without instruments, charts, or GPS, for thousands of years.

The technique inverts the Western model of navigation. In Western navigation, you are a moving point on a fixed map. In etak, the navigator holds themselves still — conceptually fixed — and the world repositions around them. A third reference island, not on the route, is chosen. The navigator tracks how that island appears to move under different stars as the voyage progresses. Each shift in the reference island's bearing marks progress along the journey. When the final star alignment is reached, landfall is near.

**The product applies this principle at the scale of a product organization.** The customer problem is the fixed reference point. Your work — your experiments, your shipped features, your validated assumptions — repositions around it. You don't follow a roadmap. You maintain orientation.

This is a name that rewards discovery. The more you know about it, the more right it feels.

### Additional context

In 1983, engineer Stan Honey — while navigating a racing yacht across the Pacific — recognized the etak principle and built it into the first commercial car navigation system, which he named Etak. The Etak Navigator, released in 1985, beat GPS to market by a decade. The technology it developed became foundational to modern digital mapping.

The 1985 company is long defunct. The name is available. And there is a precedent for commercial use that connects us directly to the history of navigation technology.

## Brand Character

**Mastery over speed.** This is not a tool for moving faster. It is a tool for moving in the right direction. The brand should feel considered and expert, not energetic or urgent.

**Craft and intelligence.** The product amplifies human judgment — it doesn't replace it. The brand should feel like it respects the person using it.

**Serious enterprise tool.** Not a consumer app. Not a side project. Not a generic AI startup with a glowing orb logo. The reference class is tools that serious engineering and product teams use every day: Linear, Figma, Notion, Vercel. The brand should sit confidently in that register while being distinctly its own thing.

**Depth that rewards discovery.** The name has a story. The mark should too — something that feels inevitable once you understand what it means, rather than something that explains itself immediately.

**What this is not.** It is not fast, bright, or playful. It is not a mapping or navigation product in the literal consumer sense. It is not a data analytics tool. It is not a project management tool in the Jira/Asana sense. If the mark looks like it could belong to any of those categories, it is wrong.

## Mark Directions

Two conceptual directions are worth exploring. They are not the only possibilities — they are the two we find most promising, and we want to see what a designer does with them. We are genuinely open to a third direction if you see one that is stronger.

### Direction 1: The Reference Island

The geometric heart of etak: a fixed reference point, and two other points in relationship around it.

The navigator is the fixed point — the observer who holds still. The destination and the reference island orbit around them. Three nodes, asymmetrically arranged, connected by lines. One node is clearly dominant — the fixed observer. The other two are secondary.

This geometry is also the product's architecture: the customer problem is the fixed point; your experiments and shipped work reposition around it.

**What we're looking for:** A mark that reads as precise and navigational, not as a generic network or graph diagram. The lines should feel like instrument precision — the geometry of a navigator's chart — not like a data startup's logo. The asymmetry is essential: symmetric arrangements read as generic. The dominant node should be unmistakably dominant.

**What we're not looking for:** Heavy filled circles that look like bullet points or loading indicators. Equal-weight nodes that flatten the hierarchy. Anything that could be a social network logo.

---

### Direction 2: The Star Compass

The Hawaiian star compass — the mental construct that Polynesian navigators used to divide the horizon into 32 houses, each marking where a celestial body rises or sets — is one of the most elegant knowledge systems ever developed. It is not a physical object. It is a structure held in the mind and applied to the world.

An abstracted version of this: a circle, representing the horizon. Radiating marks at asymmetric intervals, representing bearing points — places on the horizon where specific stars rise or set. Not equal divisions. Not a clock. Not a compass rose with N/S/E/W. The intervals are unequal because the stars are unequal.

**What we're looking for:** A mark that feels like a navigator's instrument — precise, purposeful, made by someone who knows exactly what each mark means. The circle may be partial or suggested rather than fully closed. The asymmetry is not decorative; it represents a specific reading of the sky.

**What we're not looking for:** A sun icon. A settings or gear icon. A ship's wheel. A compass rose. Anything that looks like it belongs on a consumer app or a weather widget. The failure mode here is symmetry — equal spokes produce a decorative motif, not a navigation instrument.

## Wordmark

The wordmark should be **ETAK** in all capitals.

We explored this extensively and landed on two directions worth considering:

**Direction A — Geometric sans-serif.** Something in the register of Inter, Neue Haas Grotesk, or ABC Diatype. Medium-light weight. Open letter-spacing (approximately 0.15em). Not Futura — too retro-institutional. Not rounded — too consumer. The goal is precision without coldness.

**Direction B — Transitional serif.** Something in the Canela or Freight Display register — bracketed serifs, moderate stroke contrast, editorial quality. All caps with moderate tracking (~0.075em). This is the more unexpected choice in the software space, which is precisely why it's interesting. It reads as authoritative — a navigation chart title, a publication of record — rather than a tech startup performing seriousness.

We have no strong preference between these two. We are also open to a third direction if you see a font or letterform treatment that serves the concept better.

**One custom letterform idea worth considering:** the crossbar of the E lowered to align exactly with the crossbar of the A, creating a shared horizontal axis — a horizon line — running through the word. This is the kind of micro-detail that turns a wordmark into a logotype and encodes meaning directly into the letterforms. We could not get AI image generation to execute this correctly; we mention it as something worth exploring in vector.

## Composition

The mark and wordmark will typically appear together as a horizontal lockup — mark to the left, wordmark to the right. A stacked version (mark above wordmark) will also be needed for square contexts.

The mark should be approximately the height of the cap height of the wordmark in the horizontal lockup. The spacing between mark and wordmark should feel considered — not cramped, not disconnected.

The most important rendering context is the **web application navigation bar**, where the horizontal lockup will appear at approximately 120–160px wide. Optimize for this first.

## What We Are Not Asking For

- A literal navigation or map product identity
- A maritime or travel brand aesthetic
- A data analytics or dashboard product identity
- Gradients, glow effects, or drop shadows anywhere in the mark
- Animated assets (for now)
- Brand guidelines (to follow once the mark is finalized)
- Social media templates or marketing layout applications

---

## A Note on Process

We would like to see **two or three distinct concepts** before converging. We are not looking for polish at the concept stage — rough vector explorations or even sketches that communicate the idea clearly are sufficient. We want to understand the range of possibilities before committing to a direction.

If you see a third direction we haven't considered, bring it. The brief describes where our thinking has landed, not the boundaries of the solution space.
